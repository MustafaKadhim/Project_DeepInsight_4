Motion risk before treatment begins

This project asks whether a patient's planning CT contains a visible anatomical signature of prostate motion risk. The framework links pre-treatment anatomy, fiducial-tracked motion during treatment, and calibrated decision support for personalized radiotherapy workflows.

Explore the flow Read source plan

One patient, two sources of evidence

Non-technical view: the model learns from what was visible before treatment and what actually happened during treatment. The goal is not to replace clinical judgment, but to make motion risk visible earlier.

Input

Pre-treatment anatomy

Planning CT and organ masks describe the patient's bladder, rectum, prostate, seminal vesicles, and pelvic geometry before radiotherapy starts.

Observed outcome

Motion measured during treatment

Gold fiducials tracked in triggered kV imaging provide a direct record of prostate displacement across treatment fractions.

Prediction

Calibrated risk estimate

The framework produces a patient-level probability or low, intermediate, high grouping that can be tested in retrospective triage simulations.

The anatomical motion-risk phenotype

The central scientific idea is that motion risk may not come from one measurement. It may emerge from a configuration of organs, interfaces, and pelvic geometry.

Organ state

Bladder and rectum as motion context

Volumes, distension, and shape help describe the soft-tissue conditions around the prostate.

Spatial coupling

Interfaces may matter more than isolated organs

Prostate-to-rectum distance, contact area, and bladder interface maps capture how anatomy is arranged.

Patient geometry

Pelvic shape as a stable patient signature

Pelvic width, anterior-posterior diameter, and body contour can add patient-specific context.

Explainability

Predictions should be clinically interpretable

Global and local explanations can show which anatomical feature groups drive a risk score.

A model ladder, not a black box jump

The framework compares simple transparent models against more complex imaging and foundation-model representations. Each step must earn its added complexity.

Step 1

Geometric biomarkers

Transparent features from organ volumes, distances, shapes, and contact measures.

  • Logistic regression
  • Elastic net and tree baselines
  • Clinically readable benchmark
Step 2

3D CT and mask models

Image-based models test whether learned anatomy improves risk prediction.

  • CT-only input
  • Mask-only input
  • CT plus organ masks
Step 3

Foundation representations

MAISI-v2, CTFM, or related encoders may capture richer anatomical patterns.

  • Latent anatomical embeddings
  • Correlation with known geometry
  • Representation probing
Step 4

Combined triage model

The final candidate should be calibrated, validated, and useful in virtual clinical decisions.

  • Low, intermediate, high risk
  • Calibration and decision curves
  • Patient-level explanations

What success would look like

A useful model is not just accurate. It should catch high-motion patients, avoid unnecessary intensified workflows for stable patients, and remain calibrated enough for clinical discussion.
